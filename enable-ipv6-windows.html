How to Enable or Disable IPv6 in Windows 10 & 11 (Step-by-Step)

Windows Network Settings

Are you dealing with frustrating lag in your favorite online game? Or maybe your trusty old VPN suddenly stopped working after a Windows update?

The culprit might be lurking in your network adapter settings. It's called IPv6—your PC's modern "digital address" system.

Here's the thing: enabling IPv6 can unlock smoother gaming and future-proof your connection. But sometimes, disabling it is a temporary fix for buggy VPNs or network glitches.

This guide will walk you through exactly how to do both, step by step. No confusing tech jargon—just clear, friendly instructions.

Before We Start: The One Thing You MUST Know

Your Windows PC can only use IPv6 if your router and Internet Service Provider (ISP) also support it.

🔌 The Electric Car Analogy

Think of it like this: you can own a state-of-the-art electric car (your PC), but you still need charging stations on your street (your router) and power from the grid (your ISP).

If you're not sure whether your network supports it, don't worry—we'll show you how to test everything at the end.

The Ultimate Step-by-Step Guide

This method works the same way on Windows 10 and Windows 11. Let's do this together.

Step 1: Open Your Network Connections

First, we need to open the magical "Network Connections" window. Here are two easy ways:

Option A: The "Run" Trick (Fastest Method)

  • Press the Windows key + R on your keyboard (hold both down at the same time)
  • A small box called "Run" will pop up
  • Type ncpa.cpl and hit Enter

Option B: The Control Panel Route

  • Click the Start button
  • Type Control Panel and open it
  • Go to Network and Internet
  • Click Network and Sharing Center
  • On the left sidebar, click Change adapter settings

Whichever path you choose, you should now see a list of your network connections.

Step 2: Find Your Active Connection

Look for the connection you're currently using:

  • If you're on Wi-Fi, look for Wi-Fi or Wireless Network Connection
  • If you're plugged in with a cable, look for Ethernet or Local Area Connection

The active one will usually show signal bars (for Wi-Fi) or a computer icon that doesn't have a red X on it.

Step 3: Open the Properties

  • Right-click on your active connection
  • Select Properties from the menu that appears
  • If Windows asks for permission to continue, just click Yes. You're in the right place.

Step 4: Find IPv6 in the List

In the properties window, scroll down through the list of items. You're looking for:

Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6)

It will be somewhere in the middle of the list, usually right above or below the IPv4 version.

Step 5: Enable or Disable IPv6

To ENABLE IPv6:

  • Simply check the box next to Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6)
  • Click OK at the bottom

To DISABLE IPv6:

  • Uncheck the box next to Internet Protocol Version 6 (TCP/IPv6)
  • A warning might pop up—don't worry, just click Yes
  • Click OK at the bottom

That's it! You've just changed your IPv6 settings.

⚠️ Important Warning: Should You Disable IPv6?

Microsoft strongly recommends keeping IPv6 enabled.

Why? Because IPv6 isn't some experimental feature—it's a core part of modern Windows. Disabling it can actually cause problems, including:

  • Slower network performance
  • Some apps failing to connect properly
  • Issues with Windows features like DirectAccess or BitLocker

When is it okay to disable IPv6? Only as a temporary troubleshooting step. For example:

  • Testing if an old VPN works better with IPv6 off
  • Diagnosing a specific network bug recommended by tech support
  • Following instructions from your IT department

If you disabled IPv6 to fix something, and it didn't help—or if the problem went away but you want the benefits of IPv6 back—simply follow the steps above and re-enable it.

Quick Troubleshooting Tips

"I enabled IPv6, but the checker says I don't have it!"

Remember that prerequisite check? Your ISP or router might not support IPv6 yet. Try these steps:

  • Restart your router (unplug it for 30 seconds, then plug it back in)
  • Log into your router's settings and look for IPv6—make sure it's enabled
  • Contact your ISP and ask if they offer IPv6 in your area

"I disabled IPv6, but now my internet feels slower!"

That's possible! IPv6 is designed to be more efficient. Try re-enabling it and see if performance improves.
