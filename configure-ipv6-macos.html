How to Enable, Disable, or Check IPv6 on Mac

macOS Guide

Your Mac is designed to just work—and usually, it handles network settings beautifully on its own. But sometimes, you need to peek under the hood.

Maybe you're troubleshooting a finicky VPN. Or perhaps you want to ensure your MacBook is taking full advantage of your fiber internet for smoother FaceTime calls and faster gaming.

Whatever brought you here, you're in the right place. Let's look at how macOS handles IPv6—and how you can take control when you need to.

Before You Start: The One Thing to Know

Your Mac can only use IPv6 if your Wi-Fi router and Internet Service Provider (ISP) also support it.

Think of it like this: your Mac has a brand-new superhighway entrance, but the on-ramp (your router) and the main highway (your ISP) need to connect to it.

If you're not sure whether your network is ready, stick around—we'll show you how to test everything at the end.

The Step-by-Step Guide (macOS Ventura, Sonoma, and Newer)

Apple recently redesigned System Settings. Don't worry—we'll cover both the new and old versions.

Step 1: Open System Settings

  • Click the Apple menu  in the top-left corner of your screen
  • Select System Settings

Step 2: Find Your Network

  • In the left sidebar, click Network
  • You'll see your active connections—look for Wi-Fi or Ethernet
  • Click on the connection you're currently using

Step 3: Access Connection Details

  • Look for the Details... button (it's next to the connection name)
  • Click it

Step 4: Find the TCP/IP Tab

  • In the new window, look at the top row of tabs
  • Click TCP/IP

This is the command center for all your Mac's IP settings.

Step 5: Configure IPv6

You'll see a dropdown menu labeled Configure IPv6.

To ENABLE IPv6 (Recommended):

  • Select Automatically from the dropdown
  • Click OK
  • Click Apply to save your changes

To DISABLE IPv6 (Troubleshooting only):

  • Select Off from the dropdown
  • Click OK
  • Click Apply to save your changes

That's it. Your Mac will now use your new settings immediately.

For Older macOS Versions (Monterey and Earlier)

Using an older Mac? The steps are slightly different but just as simple.

Step 1: Open System Preferences

  • Click the Apple menu 
  • Select System Preferences

Step 2: Go to Network

  • Click the Network icon

Step 3: Select Your Connection

  • In the left sidebar, choose your active connection (Wi-Fi or Ethernet)
  • Click the Advanced... button in the bottom-right corner

Step 4: Go to TCP/IP

  • Click the TCP/IP tab at the top

Step 5: Configure IPv6

  • Find the Configure IPv6 dropdown
  • Select Automatically (to enable) or Off (to disable)
  • Click OK
  • Click Apply

Perfect. You're all set.

🔍 What's That "fe80::" Address?

If you're looking at your TCP/IP settings and see an address starting with fe80::, don't panic. That's completely normal.

This is called a link-local address. Think of it as your Mac's "house number" within your own neighborhood—it only works inside your home network.

Your public IPv6 address (the one the wider internet sees) will look different. It usually starts with 2 or 3, like 2001:db8::1.

If you only see the fe80:: address, it means your Mac isn't getting a public IPv6 address from your router or ISP yet.

Why Would You Change These Settings?

When to enable IPv6:

  • You want faster, more direct connections for gaming
  • You're setting up a modern smart home
  • You just want your Mac to be future-ready

When to disable IPv6 (temporary only):

  • You're troubleshooting a VPN that's acting up
  • A specific app or game isn't connecting properly
  • Your IT department or tech support asked you to test it

Important: Apple keeps IPv6 enabled by default for good reason. Only disable it as a temporary test, and turn it back on when you're done.

Quick Troubleshooting Tips

"I set IPv6 to Automatically, but the checker says I don't have it!"

Your router or ISP might not support IPv6 yet. Try these:

  • Restart your router (unplug for 30 seconds, plug back in)
  • Log into your router's settings and look for IPv6—make sure it's enabled
  • Contact your ISP and ask if they offer IPv6 in your area

"I disabled IPv6, but now some websites feel slower!"

That can happen! IPv6 routes data more efficiently. Try re-enabling it and see if performance improves.
