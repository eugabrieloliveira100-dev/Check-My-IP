How Does a VPN Hide Your IP Address?

VPN & Security

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) hides your IP address by creating an encrypted tunnel between your device and a VPN server, then routing all your internet traffic through that server. Websites and online services see the VPN server's IP address instead of your real one.

The Technical Process: Step by Step

1. Connection Establishment

When you connect to a VPN service, your device establishes a secure, encrypted connection to a VPN server operated by the VPN provider. This connection uses protocols like WireGuard, OpenVPN, or IKEv2/IPsec.

2. IP Address Replacement

Once connected, all your internet traffic is routed through the VPN server. Websites and online services see the VPN server's IP address instead of your actual IP address.

3. Encryption Layer

VPNs use strong encryption (typically AES-256) to protect your data. This means:

  • Your ISP can't see which websites you visit
  • Your data is protected from interception on public Wi-Fi
  • Your online activities are hidden from network observers

Types of VPN Protocols

WireGuard

A modern VPN protocol known for its simplicity, speed, and strong security. Uses state-of-the-art cryptography and has a smaller codebase than older protocols.

OpenVPN

An open-source protocol that's highly configurable and considered very secure. Can bypass most firewalls and works on almost any platform.

IKEv2/IPsec

Particularly good for mobile devices as it handles network changes well (switching from Wi-Fi to cellular data). Developed by Microsoft and Cisco.

What Your ISP Can Still See

While a VPN hides your browsing activity, your ISP can still see:

  • That you're connected to a VPN server
  • The amount of data transferred (but not the content)
  • The VPN server's IP address
  • The timing of your connections

Limitations of VPN IP Hiding

DNS Leaks

If your device uses your ISP's DNS servers instead of the VPN's, websites can still determine your approximate location through DNS queries.

WebRTC Leaks

WebRTC (Web Real-Time Communication) in browsers can sometimes reveal your real IP address even when using a VPN.

Browser Fingerprinting

Websites can use browser fingerprinting techniques that don't rely on IP addresses to track you across sessions.

VPN vs Other Privacy Tools

Comparison: VPN vs Proxy vs Tor
VPN: Encrypts ALL traffic, hides IP, medium speed
Proxy: Routes traffic, may hide IP, no encryption
Tor: Multiple encryption layers, very slow, maximum anonymity

Best Practices for VPN Use

  1. Choose a reputable provider: Look for transparent no-logs policies and independent audits
  2. Enable kill switch: Prevents data leaks if VPN connection drops
  3. Use DNS leak protection: Ensure your VPN handles DNS queries properly
  4. Regularly test for leaks: Use tools to verify no IP or DNS leaks
  5. Keep software updated: Ensure your VPN client has the latest security patches

Technical Considerations

When evaluating VPNs technically, consider:

  • Encryption strength: AES-256 is current standard
  • Protocol options: Multiple protocol support is better
  • Server locations: More locations provide better options
  • Connection logs: True no-logging policy is essential for privacy
  • Jurisdiction: Provider's legal jurisdiction affects data retention laws
