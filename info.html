What is an IP Address? The Concept of Digital Identity

To understand what an IP (Internet Protocol) address is, forget about computers for a second and think about the postal system. If you want to send a letter to a friend, you need an address. Without it, the mail carrier has no idea where to deliver the envelope. On the internet, every deviceâ€”whether it's your smartphone, laptop, or a gaming serverâ€”needs a similar address so that data knows where to go.1 An IP address is exactly that: a numerical label assigned to each device connected to a network that uses the Internet Protocol for communication.

However, there's a nuance that often trips people up: the difference between who you are and where you are. In technical terms, an IP address serves two main functions. First, it identifies the host (the device). Second, it provides the logical location of that host on the network so routers can find the best path to deliver data packets.3 I often call the IP a "connection identity." If you take your laptop from your house to a coffee shop, your physical identity remains the same, but your digital connection identity changes because you are now at a new entry point in the global network.4

The internet isn't some amorphous cloud; it's a massive physical infrastructure of cables, satellites, and routers. The IP protocol is the set of rules governing how data is addressed and routed through this infrastructure. Every time you load a website, your device is essentially shouting, "Hey, I'm IP X, and I want the data from IP Y!" The website responds by sending small chunks of information, called packets, which carry your IP address as the destination.1 Without this structure, digital communication would simply collapse.
